Karen's Journal,Karen Gravorn
{p}
-- Inside Cover --

This record is not an official document of the Order. As a Knight Commander, I reviewed the reports in the archives and added my personal findings and observations.

The official records state that "all successors were evil." But that is not true.

-- Karen Gravorn
{p}
-- The First (Name Unknown) --

In the deepest layer of the archives, fragmentary records survive -- barely legible. They mention only "the first owner of the forbidden tome." Not even a name survives.

The official position is that "an evil sorcerer created the tome." But upon close reading, there is one passage that suggests the tome was not "created" but "found." This contradicts the official account.
{p}
Tracing the dates of the records, the first appears to have lived in the previous age -- the end of the so-called "Age of Calamity." Order records from that period reference the "Great Pestilence." A plague born of bacterial rampage. The devastation was immense, and conventional healing arts proved helpless.

In records from the same period, there are fragmentary references to a "herbalist" -- praised as a dedicated healer in one passage, yet mentioned in connection with crimes of profanity in another. Whether this "herbalist" is the same person as the first is unconfirmed.
{p}
But the record of the ending is -- strange. It says "subjugated," but the details are absent. Only the final lines of the report, in a different tone, suggest "transformation." The first did not die. They "changed."

Scrawled in the margins, in a different hand: the words "apotheosis" and "transcendence." Not part of the formal report -- someone added them later.
{p}
Piecing the fragments together, the first may have ceased to be human. Became a god of the dead -- or something close to it. But their identity is completely lost.

All that remains is a record of "danger." After the first's "transformation," the tome is said to have begun choosing its next owner of its own accord.

Assessment: If the first was indeed a healer from the age of the Great Pestilence, their motive for reaching for the tome may have been to find a cure. But there is no proof. What is certain: the second through fifth all met ruin. And the first alone may have "succeeded." Succeeded, and vanished from human history. -- That is the most frightening possibility.
{p}
The Second, Valdis. A healer and researcher.

He turned to the tome to save his wife from an incurable disease.

The resurrection succeeded. But the wife who returned was "something else." Valdis himself acknowledged this and surrendered to the Order voluntarily.

Official position: "A man driven mad by the tome."

Assessment: Records of his interrogation upon surrender survive. His responses were lucid. Madmen do not surrender of their own accord. He accepted the consequences of his actions while fully sane. -- That is not madness. It is despair.
{p}
The Third, Selyu. A scholar.

The first person to systematically organize the tome's theoretical framework.

As personality drift progressed, in the final stages he could no longer distinguish whether he was the first or the third. He was defeated while repeatedly asking, "Who am I?"

Official position: "A textbook case of mental contamination."

Assessment: Selyu's research notes are stored in the archives. The content is logical and consistent. Not the writing of a madman. ...But perhaps being logical is itself the tome's influence. I cannot tell where the line is.
{p}
The Fourth, Mireille. A former cleric.

She attempted to study the tome from within the Jure order.

She advocated "resurrection that preserves the dignity of the dead" and established her own ethical code for the treatment of servants. As personality drift progressed, the memories and emotions of her predecessors became intermixed with her own.

In the end, she was said to have asked, through tears, "Is this my emotion?"

Official position: "A traitor who betrayed the order."

Assessment: I can understand Mireille's ideals. The conviction that the dead should not be treated as tools. But no matter how noble the ideal, it could not stop the tome's erosion. Ideals are no shield.
{p}
The Fifth, Erenos. The only successor I confronted directly.

The reason the Order pursued Erenos was simple. He possessed a proscribed artifact and practiced necromancy, raising servants.

At least twelve servants confirmed. We assaulted Erenos's stronghold three times; the first two attempts ended in failure.

Erenos did not kill us. He found a more efficient method than killing.

He had prisoners read a passage from the tome, making them "understand." Of our number, seven defected through persuasion.
{p}
My subordinate Alvin was the foremost among them. An excellent swordsman and loyal soldier. After reading the tome for three days, he left the Order of his own will. He was smiling. He did not understand what he had lost -- or perhaps he understood, and no longer considered it a loss. I no longer have the means to determine which is true.

Erenos was ultimately defeated. But his perspective remains within the tome. It will be passed on to the next successor.

Assessment: Erenos was the most dangerous of the five. Because he did not rely on violence. Violence can be countered. "Understanding" -- cannot.
{p}
-- Reflections --

All five began with good intentions. All five believed they were different. All five arrived at the same place.

When the sixth appears, I intend not only to secure the tome, but to hand over this journal. Not the official records -- but the truth.

I have no intention of repeating this game of cat and mouse with the tome any longer. To break the chain, the successor's own cooperation is necessary.
